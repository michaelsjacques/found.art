The Case for Original Art

Owning art is one
of the most human
things you can do.

It’s not about investment returns or impressing anyone. It’s about surrounding yourself with things made by other humans, for no practical reason at all — just feeling and expression.

01

Your money goes straight to the artist

When you buy from a gallery, the artist gets about half. Big platforms? Same deal. But buy direct from an artist’s website or Instagram, and almost all of it reaches them. That means your purchase literally pays someone’s rent and lets them keep making work. It’s one of the most direct ways to support something you believe in.

02

You get something nobody else has

An original painting or drawing is a one-of-a-kind object — not a print, not a copy. The actual thing someone made with their hands. You can own something that exists exactly once in the world. No one else can have it. That’s not a marketing pitch, it’s just genuinely true.

03

Your home starts to feel like yours

Ever walked into someone’s home and immediately got a sense of who they are? That’s art doing that. The pieces you choose say something about what moves you and what you think is beautiful. It’s one of the easiest ways to make a space feel genuinely personal rather than just furnished.

04

You’re getting in early — and that matters

Many artists selling for $500–$1,500 today will be significantly pricier in a few years as their careers grow. You’re not speculating — you’re buying work you love while it’s still accessible. People who started collecting early always say the same thing: “I wish I’d bought more when I had the chance.”

05

You start seeing the world differently

This sounds a bit much, but it’s genuinely true: living with art you love changes how you notice things. Light, colour, composition start jumping out everywhere. You stop scrolling past beautiful things. It’s a bit like learning a new language — once you start paying attention, the world gets more interesting.

06

It can actually hold & grow in value

We’re not promising you’ll flip paintings for profit — that’s not the point. But works by artists with growing careers do tend to hold their value and often increase over time. The upside vs stocks or real estate? You get to live with your investment and enjoy looking at it every single day.

Another Way to Collect

Art auctions:
demystified.

Auctions aren't just for Christie's and Sotheby's. There are specialist houses focused on accessible, emerging, and regional art — and you can bid online from anywhere. Here's how it works and where to look.

01
What actually happens at an auction

An auction house gathers works from collectors or estates and sells them to the highest bidder. You register, set a maximum bid, and either attend in person or bid online in real time. The auction house takes a "buyer's premium" (usually 15–25%) on top of the hammer price — factor that into your budget.

02
Why auctions can be a great deal

Works sometimes sell below their estimated value if fewer bidders show up that day. Estates and collection sales often have strong works at fair prices. It's also one of the best ways to find established artists at prices that feel more like emerging — because the original market excitement has passed.

03
What to watch out for

Always preview the work in person or request a condition report before bidding. Factor in the buyer's premium before you fall in love with a price. Set your maximum bid in advance and stick to it — auction excitement is real. And check provenance carefully on higher-value lots.

Tips for your first auction

Buying at auction feels intimidating the first time. It isn't — once you know the mechanics.

Budget
Always include the buyer's premium

If a work hammers at $800, you'll pay $800 plus 20–25% in fees. Budget $1,000 for an $800 lot. It's not a trick — it's just how auctions work. Every house lists their premium schedule on their website.

Research
Look up the artist's price history first

Before you bid, search the artist on Artsy or LiveAuctioneers to see what their work has sold for recently. If the estimate seems low, that's either a bargain or a sign something's off — worth investigating.

Condition
Request a condition report

Every reputable house will provide a written condition report on request. Read it. Restoration, damage, and fading all affect value. Don't bid on a lot you haven't seen in person or received a thorough condition report for.

Strategy
Set your max and stick to it

Decide your maximum before the auction opens, not during it. The pace and competition of live bidding creates real pressure to go higher. Your pre-set maximum is your rational brain. Listen to it more than your gut in the room.
