Why collect
original art?

01

It lives with you

A print is decoration. An original is a relationship. Every morning you see it differently — because you are different. Art that was made by a human hand has a presence that reproductions don't.

02

Emerging artists are affordable

The most interesting work in the market right now costs between $500 and $5,000. That's where careers are being made and where collectors with taste — not just capital — win.

03

It tends to hold value

Art bought from the right emerging artists at the right moment has a track record of appreciating. That's not the primary reason to collect — but it's not a reason against it either.

04

You support a person

When you buy an original, you're not paying a corporation's margin. You're paying an artist's rent, studio costs, and the time they spend making something only they could make.

05

The market rewards early

Collectors who bought Cecily Brown or Jonas Wood at the beginning of their careers paid a fraction of today's prices. That window exists for many artists working right now.

06

It starts a conversation

Good art prompts questions. Who made this? Why? What's it about? The piece on your wall becomes an entry point into a larger world — artists, ideas, movements, history.

“Buy with your eyes, not your ears. Trust what you’re drawn to most.”

Another Way to Collect

Art auctions:
demystified.

Auctions aren't just for Christie's and Sotheby's. There are specialist houses focused on accessible, emerging, and regional art — and you can bid online from anywhere. Here's how it works and where to look.

01
What actually happens at an auction

An auction house gathers works from collectors or estates and sells them to the highest bidder. You register, set a maximum bid, and either attend in person or bid online in real time. The auction house takes a "buyer's premium" (usually 15–25%) on top of the hammer price — factor that into your budget.

02
Why auctions can be a great deal

Works sometimes sell below their estimated value if fewer bidders show up that day. Estates and collection sales often have strong works at fa

the original market excitement has passed.

03
What to watch out for

Always preview the work in person or request a condition report before bidding. Factor in the buyer's premium before you fall in love with a price. Set your maximum bid in advance and stick to it — auction excitement is real. And check provenance carefully on higher-value lots.

Tips for your first auction

Buying at auction feels intimidating the first time. It isn't — once you know the mechanics.

Budget
Always include the buyer's premium

If a work hammers at $800, you'll pay $800 plus 20–25% in fees. Budget $1,000 for an $800 lot. It's not a trick — it's just how auctions work. Every house lists their premium schedule on their website.

Research
Look up the artist's price history first

Before you bid, search the artist on Artsy or LiveAuctioneers to see what their work has sold for recently. If the estimate seems low, that's either a bargain or a sign something's off — worth investigating.

Condition
Request a condition report

Every reputable house will provide a written condition report on request. Read it. Restoration, damage, and fading all affect value. Don't bid on a lot you haven't seen in person or received a thorough condition report for.

Strategy
Set your max and stick to it

Decide your maximum before the auction opens, not during it. The pace and competition of live bidding creates real pressure to go higher. Your pre-set maximum is your rational brain. Listen to it more than your gut in the room.
